How to Choose a Tradesperson in New Zealand

Finding a good tradesperson in New Zealand can feel like a lottery. You've probably heard the horror stories — jobs half-finished, bills that doubled from the quote, or "tradespeople" who turned out to have no relevant qualifications at all. As someone who spent 15 years on the tools before starting PeakHome, I can tell you what actually matters when hiring someone to work on your home.

Check their licence — and verify it

In New Zealand, certain trades require a government licence to operate legally. Plumbers, gasfitters, and drainlayers must hold a current licence from the Plumbers, Gasfitters and Drainlayers Board (PGDB). Electricians must be registered with the Electrical Workers Registration Board (EWRB). For both, you can verify a licence online in under 60 seconds.

Roofing, painting, landscaping, and general maintenance don't require a government licence — but they should still carry public liability insurance (more on that below). For any licensed trade, never take a verbal assurance at face value. Look the person up on the relevant register before work starts.

Ask for proof of public liability insurance

Public liability insurance protects you if the tradesperson damages your property or injures someone while on your property. Without it, you could be left covering costs that aren't your fault. Any professional tradesperson should be able to produce a current certificate of currency without hesitation. If they can't — or make excuses about it — walk away.

The minimum cover you'd want to see for residential work is $1 million. Larger or more complex jobs warrant higher cover. Don't be embarrassed to ask — it's standard practice.

Get at least two written quotes

A written quote protects you far better than a verbal one. It should clearly state the scope of work, the labour rate or fixed price, materials to be used (and whether they're included), and the expected timeline. Watch for quotes that are vague about scope — "fix the roof issue" is not a quote; "replace 4m of damaged flashing around the chimney, including materials" is.

Getting two quotes lets you sense-check the price and the approach. A dramatically cheaper quote isn't always better — it might mean cutting corners on materials or missing part of the job. What you want is a quote that's detailed, realistic, and explained to you clearly.

Check references and reviews

Ask for two or three recent references from similar jobs. A tradesperson who's proud of their work will have no trouble providing them. Call the references and ask: Did they show up when they said they would? Was the finished job what you expected? Were there any surprises on the invoice? Would you use them again?

Google reviews and local Facebook groups are also useful — but be aware that a business with 4 reviews isn't necessarily worse than one with 200. Look for specificity in the reviews. "Great work, highly recommend" tells you almost nothing. "Replaced our entire bathroom in 4 days, came in on budget, fixed a drainage issue they spotted while on site" tells you a lot.

Red flags to watch for

  • Asking for a large upfront cash payment before work starts
  • Reluctance to provide a written quote
  • No fixed business address or NZBN/GST number
  • Pressure to decide immediately or "the price goes up tomorrow"
  • Can't name their insurance provider
  • No online presence at all — no website, no reviews, no social media

Know what the job should cost

You don't need to know exact prices, but a rough sense of market rates helps you spot outliers. In Auckland, expect to pay $85–$120 per hour for licensed plumbers and electricians, and $70–$90 for painters and landscapers. Significantly below this range usually means something's been cut — either qualifications, insurance, or the actual scope of the work.
